Badminton is a fast-paced and exhilarating racket sport that demands a unique blend of agility, precision, and explosive power. Played on a rectangular court divided by a high net, the game involves hitting a shuttlecock—a feathered projectile with distinct aerodynamic properties—back and forth. Unlike ball sports, the shuttlecock’s drag causes it to decelerate rapidly, forcing players to master a variety of techniques, from delicate net tumbles to thunderous overhead smashes. Whether played in singles or doubles, the sport requires intense focus and quick reflexes, as the shuttlecock can reach speeds of over 400 km/h in professional matches. Beyond the physical challenge, badminton is a highly strategic "chess game" played at high speed. Players must constantly outmaneuver their opponents by using clever footwork and deceptive shots to create openings in the defense. It provides an excellent cardiovascular workout, improving heart health, flexibility, and coordination. Because it is relatively easy to learn but difficult to master, badminton has become one of the most popular sports globally, enjoyed by millions as both a casual backyard pastime and a prestigious Olympic event.

The Serving Rule (Underhand Only)
All serves must be delivered with an underhand stroke, ensuring the shuttlecock is struck below the server's waist. Additionally, the serve must travel diagonally across the net and land within the opponent's designated service box to be considered legal.

The "One Hit" Rule
A player or team is allowed only one contact with the shuttlecock to send it over the net to the opposing side. If you hit the shuttlecock twice or if both partners in doubles touch it during the same turn, the rally ends and the point is awarded to the opponent.

The Net Rule
Players and their rackets are strictly prohibited from touching the net or its supports while the shuttlecock is in play. If any part of your body, clothing, or racket makes contact with the net before the rally is officially over, you commit a fault and lose the point.
